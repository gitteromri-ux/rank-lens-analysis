Executive Summary

Where does the call center perform best?

We analyzed every rep-week across the two core schools — Hebrew Related and Biblical Related — pairing each rep's category rank (top performers = 3–4) with the call-center's weighted SOV. The answer is counterintuitive: more rank 3–4 is not always better.

Methodology

How the sweet spot was found

01

Weekly join

Each rep-category-week in the rank file contains a trailing 10-week volume and a quartile rank (1 = low, 4 = top). We aggregated these to compute the share of contacted handled by rank 3–4 reps in each school per week.

02

SOV alignment

Daily rep-level SOV (weighted by contacted volume) was rolled up to a weekly call-center-wide figure and smoothed on a 10-week trailing window to align with the rank window.

03

Curve fitting

For each school we fit a Gaussian-kernel smoother over the rank-3–4 share × SOV pairs, with a quadratic reference to identify the peak and shape (monotonic vs inverted-U).

04

Sweet spot

Optimal range = smoothed SOV ≥ 97 % of peak. Bad threshold = share values where smoothed SOV drops below the call-center's 25th-percentile SOV (or the mid-point between peak and trough).

05

Significance

Spearman ρ and Pearson r tested two-sided. A "strong" signal = |ρ| > 0.5 with p < 0.01. "Directional" = 0.2 < |ρ| ≤ 0.5. "Weak" = |ρ| ≤ 0.2. Categories with fewer than 10 weeks were excluded.

06

Caveats

The rank share is driven by trailing volume — a 100 % reading often signals that rank 1–2 reps weren't active that week, not that top reps did more. Optimum recommendations should be read as "who is carrying the phone", not pure performance.
